Dissecting The Life of Pablo, Pt. 1: Kanye West – Famous
Published on July 29, 2019 by Sahaj Amatya
           

Brief notice before reading this blog:
The links are set so that the video jumps to the exact timestamp where I want the reader to start their observation.

As soon as the record starts spinning, you hear Rihanna (uncredited) sing the lyrics from the 1968 classic Do What You Gotta Do by Nina Simone. The lyrics go as:

Man, I can understand how it might be
Kind of hard to love a girl like me
I don’t blame you much for wanting to be free
I just wanted you to know
I loved you better than your own kin did
From the very start
It's my own fault for what happens to my heart
You see I've always known you'd go

It is a song from a woman hesitantly granting freedom to a man who has apparently grown distant from her. She is accepting that he would be better off on his own in search of liberty than to be felt forced into a relationship with her that would prevent the fruition of his full potential.

The song then transitions into a macabre vibe with the addition of a sample from Rovescio della Medaglia’s 1973 piece Mi sono svegliato e… ho chiuso gli occhi from the album Contaminazione di alcune idee de certi preludi e fughe del Clavicembalo ben temperato di J. S. Bach. I don’t blame you if you skipped over that whole flurry of gibberish. Translated from Italian, it means “Contamination of some ideas of certain preludes and fugues of the well-tempered Clavier by J. S. Bach.” The album explores pieces from Bach through a modern perspective.

Then we are acquainted with the infamous egotistical side of Kanye via the first verse where he goes on to lay down how he is in control over making other people famous. Personally, like most people, I am neither a fan of the lyrics nor the delivery of the verses.

What I am a fan of though, is the layers of audio that are placed very meticulously. If you utilize a decent audio output device you may observe that when the timestamp hits 1:50:

you hear a muffled voice (Kanye) singing the verse to Nina Simone’s Do What You Gotta Do muted by the sample of 1982’s reggae anthem, Bam Bam by Sister Nancy.

The track with Kanye’s voice only becomes fully audible at 2:53.

The human voice is used as the instrument that carries the song along its most important part: the beat drop.

This song is an amalgamation of several timeless pieces of musical art throughout the decades. When thought about, the diversity of the pieces used is astounding, we have late 60’s jazz, we have 18th century Bach brought back to life in the 1970’s, and we have reggae all tied up by modern hip hop to produce this 3 minute and 16 second piece of audio.

It is sad that such pieces of art are overlooked because of the superficial controversy surrounding it. Maybe it was intended to be so, even when observing the album cover, one might question regarding who would take an album seriously when it advertises a woman’s fake posterior so explicitly? Who would take a song seriously when the lyrics start off with the singer stating the sexual possibilities with another artist in the most unsophisticated and falsely incentivized way possible?

I suppose it is to be expected that we turn our heads away when faced with the undesirable side of an artist to appreciate the side that is relevant to us.
